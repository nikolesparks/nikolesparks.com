Services / Therapy for anxiety

Your anxiety is trying to tell you something.

Anxiety is exhausting to live with — especially when you can't figure out what it's actually about. It shows up uninvited, doesn't respond to logic, and makes everything feel more uncertain. But anxiety is a signal. And signals, when we learn to read them, have something worth hearing.

What anxiety can look like

Anxiety doesn't always announce itself as anxiety.

For many people it isn't the dramatic, acute kind. It's quieter and more pervasive.

01A persistent hum of worry you can't quite turn off
02Difficulty trusting what is good: relationships, praise, moments of rest and peace
03A strong need for control or routine that makes spontaneity feel impossible
04High standards and relentless achievement driven more by fear of failure than genuine desire
05Overthinking decisions or replaying conversations afterward
06Difficulty relaxing, even when nothing is actually wrong
07Procrastination, avoidance, or staying very busy to outrun a feeling
08Feeling like an imposter, waiting to be found out

Many of the people I work with have been anxious for so long that it feels like just who they are. It doesn't have to be. Anxiety is a pattern that developed for reasons — and those reasons are worth understanding.

How I work with anxiety

Curiosity, not urgency.

My work draws on psychodynamic and attachment-based principles, where anxiety is understood to have a specific relationship to underlying feeling. When a genuine emotion begins to rise — particularly one that has been historically dangerous to experience — anxiety is what intercepts it. Rather than focusing on coping skills or thought restructuring, we go a layer deeper: what is your anxiety signaling, how did early relational experiences shape your nervous system's sense of what is safe, and what feelings might be living just out of awareness.

Beneath anxiety, there is almost always something else — grief that never found a place to land, anger that felt too dangerous to express, longing that carries an old memory of loss. The goal isn't to make you someone who never feels anxious. It's to develop a different relationship with anxiety — one where you can hear what it's trying to say, rather than just trying to make it stop.

Frequently asked questions

Can therapy actually help with anxiety?+

Yes, and the research supports this. Psychodynamic therapy has a solid evidence base for treating anxiety, and it tends to produce changes that hold — because it addresses the underlying patterns rather than just the surface symptoms. What helps depends on what's driving your anxiety, which is one of the things we'd explore together.

What if my anxiety isn't "bad enough" for therapy?+

Anxiety doesn't have to be debilitating to be worth addressing. If it's affecting your relationships, your work, your ability to rest, or the life you want to pursue — that's enough. A lot of people wait until anxiety has gotten very loud before seeking help. You don't have to wait.

Is psychodynamic anxiety therapy different from CBT?+

CBT works by identifying and changing anxious thought patterns and behaviors in the present — useful, especially for specific, well-defined triggers. Psychodynamic therapy works differently: rather than managing anxiety at the surface, it explores the underlying emotional patterns, relational histories, and character structure generating it. For people whose anxiety keeps returning despite coping strategies, depth-oriented work often reaches something CBT does not.

Will we talk about my past?+

Often, yes — though not in a way that feels disconnected from your present life. Attachment-based therapy is interested in how early experiences shaped the patterns you're living out now. For many people, understanding where their anxiety was formed, and what it was originally responding to, is what finally makes change possible. We always follow your pace and readiness.

How long does anxiety therapy take?+

That depends on the depth and roots of the anxiety. I typically work with people in longer-term therapy of at least six months, because lasting change takes time. Some people notice significant shifts within a few months; for others the work unfolds more gradually. How you're feeling about the work is an ongoing, collaborative conversation between us.

Do you offer online anxiety therapy in California?+

Yes. I offer telehealth sessions for anyone located in California through a secure video platform. Online anxiety therapy works very similarly to in-person sessions, and many clients find it equally valuable.
